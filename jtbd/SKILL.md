# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) Framework

## Core Insight: The Milkshake Story

Customers don't buy products — they **hire** solutions to get a job done.

Clayton Christensen's famous milkshake study: A fast-food chain wanted to sell more milkshakes. Demographics told them nothing. But when they asked "What job are you hiring the milkshake to do?", they discovered two jobs:
- **Morning commuters** hired a thick milkshake to make a boring drive interesting and stay full until lunch (competing with bagels, bananas, donuts)
- **Evening parents** hired a milkshake to be a treat for their kids (competing with toys, promises, ice cream)

Same product, two completely different jobs. The improvements for each job contradicted each other.

**Key principle**: The unit of analysis is the **job**, not the customer or the product.

## Job Statement Format

A well-formed job statement follows this structure:

> **"When** [situation/context], **I want to** [motivation/action], **so I can** [expected outcome/benefit]"

Examples:
- "When I'm commuting alone in the morning, I want something to keep me occupied and full, so I can make the drive enjoyable and not be hungry before lunch."
- "When a production line breaks down unexpectedly, I want rapid expert response, so I can minimize downtime and meet delivery commitments."

### Rules for Good Job Statements
1. **Situation first** — jobs are triggered by circumstances, not demographics
2. **Stable over time** — jobs don't change even when solutions do (people have always needed to "get from A to B")
3. **Solution-agnostic** — never mention products or technologies in the job statement
4. **Functional verb** — use action verbs (achieve, reduce, maintain, avoid, manage)

## Three Dimensions of Jobs

Every job has three layers. Most companies only address the functional layer, leaving emotional and social jobs to competitors.

### 1. Functional Jobs (기능적 직무)
The practical task the customer wants to accomplish.
- "Clean industrial tanks to specification within the maintenance window"
- "Remove sludge without damaging equipment"
- Measured by: speed, quality, cost, reliability

### 2. Emotional Jobs (감정적 직무)
How the customer wants to **feel** (or avoid feeling).
- "Feel confident that the job won't cause safety incidents"
- "Feel relieved that compliance requirements are met"
- "Avoid anxiety about hidden costs or surprise charges"
- Measured by: peace of mind, confidence, reduced stress

### 3. Social Jobs (사회적 직무)
How the customer wants to be **perceived** by others.
- "Be seen as a competent plant manager who chooses reliable contractors"
- "Demonstrate to leadership that I chose the most professional vendor"
- "Be recognized by peers as someone who gets things done efficiently"
- Measured by: reputation, professional standing, peer recognition

## Universal Job Map (8 Steps)

Every job follows a universal process. Map your customer's job across these 8 steps to find unmet needs at each stage.

```
┌──────────┐   ┌──────────┐   ┌──────────┐   ┌──────────┐
│ 1.Define │──▶│ 2.Locate │──▶│3.Prepare │──▶│4.Confirm │
│ (정의)   │   │ (탐색)   │   │ (준비)   │   │ (확인)   │
└──────────┘   └──────────┘   └──────────┘   └──────────┘
      │                                            │
      ▼                                            ▼
┌──────────┐   ┌──────────┐   ┌──────────┐   ┌──────────┐
│8.Conclude│◀──│7.Modify  │◀──│6.Monitor │◀──│5.Execute │
│ (완료)   │   │ (수정)   │   │ (모니터) │   │ (실행)   │
└──────────┘   └──────────┘   └──────────┘   └──────────┘
```

| Step | Description | Key Questions |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **1. Define (정의)** | Determine what needs to be done | What criteria matter? How do you plan? |
| **2. Locate (탐색)** | Find inputs and information needed | What do you need to gather? What's hard to find? |
| **3. Prepare (준비)** | Set up the environment and inputs | What setup is required? What causes delays? |
| **4. Confirm (확인)** | Verify readiness before execution | How do you know you're ready? What validation? |
| **5. Execute (실행)** | Perform the core job | What's the main activity? Where do errors occur? |
| **6. Monitor (모니터링)** | Track progress and quality | How do you know it's working? What do you measure? |
| **7. Modify (수정)** | Make adjustments as needed | What changes mid-course? What triggers adjustment? |
| **8. Conclude (완료)** | Finish, clean up, evaluate | How do you know it's done? What's the handoff? |

**Usage**: For each step, ask "What makes this step difficult, time-consuming, or error-prone?" — that's where innovation opportunities hide.

## Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) — Ulwick Method

Anthony Ulwick's quantitative approach to JTBD. Desired outcomes are **measurable statements** that define success for each job step.

### Desired Outcome Statement Format
> **[Direction] + [metric] + [object of control] + [contextual clarifier]**

Examples:
- "**Minimize** the **time** it takes to **identify the source of contamination** in a tank"
- "**Minimize** the **likelihood** of **equipment damage** during cleaning"
- "**Minimize** the **number of** **unplanned return visits** after maintenance"

### Opportunity Algorithm
For each outcome, survey customers on:
- **Importance** (1-10): How important is this outcome?
- **Satisfaction** (1-10): How satisfied are you with current solutions?

**Opportunity Score = Importance + max(Importance - Satisfaction, 0)**

| Score | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| > 15 | Extreme opportunity (극한 기회) — underserved, high priority |
| 12-15 | High opportunity (높은 기회) — strong unmet need |
| 10-12 | Moderate opportunity (보통 기회) — worth exploring |
| < 10 | Overserved or low priority — avoid investing here |

## Competitive Landscape Through Jobs Lens

Traditional competition analysis looks at similar products. JTBD reveals the **true competitive set**: anything hired for the same job.

### Categories of Competition
1. **Direct competitors (직접 경쟁)** — Same solution type (other industrial cleaning companies)
2. **Secondary competitors (간접 경쟁)** — Different solution, same job (in-house maintenance teams)
3. **Non-consumption (비소비)** — Customer does nothing / tolerates the problem (deferred maintenance)
4. **Workarounds (임시방편)** — Cobbled-together makeshift solutions

### Key Insight
Your biggest competitor is often **non-consumption** or **workarounds**, not the company across the street.

## Switch Timeline (전환 타임라인)

Understanding how customers switch from one solution to another. The timeline from first thought to full adoption.

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
 First       Event/      Passive     Active      Decision
 Thought     Trigger     Looking     Looking
 (첫 인식)   (계기)      (수동 탐색)  (능동 탐색)  (결정)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
                                                    │
                                                    ▼
                                      ┌───────────────────────────────┐
                                      │  Consuming    Satisfaction    │
                                      │  (사용)       (만족/불만족)   │
                                      └───────────────────────────────┘
```

| Phase | Description | Interview Questions |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **First Thought (첫 인식)** | Seed of dissatisfaction planted | When did you first think something wasn't working? |
| **Event/Trigger (촉발 사건)** | Specific event that catalyzes action | What happened that made you finally decide to look? |
| **Passive Looking (수동 탐색)** | Casual awareness, not yet committed | What did you notice without actively searching? |
| **Active Looking (능동 탐색)** | Deliberate search and comparison | What criteria did you use? Who did you ask? |
| **Decision (결정)** | Commit to switch | What tipped the balance? What almost stopped you? |
| **Consuming (사용)** | First use and ongoing experience | Did it match expectations? What surprised you? |
| **Satisfaction (만족도)** | Post-adoption evaluation | Would you hire it again? What would you change? |

## Forces of Progress Diagram (진보의 힘)

Four forces that determine whether a customer switches to a new solution:

```
        ◀══════════ DEMAND GENERATION ══════════▶

   ┌─────────────────────┐     ┌─────────────────────┐
   │  PUSH (밀어내는 힘)  │     │  PULL (끌어당기는 힘) │
   │                     │     │                     │
   │ Dissatisfaction     │ ──▶ │ Attraction of new   │
   │ with current state  │     │ solution             │
   │ 현상에 대한 불만     │     │ 새 해결책의 매력     │
   └─────────────────────┘     └─────────────────────┘

        ═══════════ vs ═══════════

   ┌─────────────────────┐     ┌─────────────────────┐
   │ ANXIETY (불안)       │     │ HABIT (관성/습관)    │
   │                     │     │                     │
   │ Fear of the new     │ ◀── │ Comfort with current │
   │ solution            │     │ way of doing things  │
   │ 새것에 대한 불안     │     │ 현재 방식의 편안함   │
   └─────────────────────┘     └─────────────────────┘

        ◀══════════ DEMAND REDUCTION ══════════▶
```

### Switching happens when:
**Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit**

### Strategic implications:
- **Increase Push**: Help customers see the cost of their current situation (data, benchmarks, risk exposure)
- **Increase Pull**: Demonstrate the specific better outcomes your solution enables
- **Reduce Anxiety**: Offer trials, guarantees, case studies, phased adoption
- **Reduce Habit**: Make switching easy, provide migration support, reduce learning curve

## Interview Techniques for Uncovering Jobs

### The Switch Interview (Bob Moesta Method)
Focus on a **specific** past purchase/hiring decision. Never ask hypotheticals.

**Timeline questions** (establish the story):
1. "Tell me about when you [bought/hired X]. Walk me through it."
2. "When did you first start thinking about it?"
3. "What was happening in your life/work that made you start looking?"
4. "What else did you consider?"
5. "What almost stopped you from making the change?"

**Energy questions** (find the forces):
6. "What was frustrating about what you had before?" (Push)
7. "What excited you about the new solution?" (Pull)
8. "What worried you about switching?" (Anxiety)
9. "What made it hard to leave the old way?" (Habit)

### Rules for JTBD Interviews
- Interview **recent** switchers (within 90 days)
- Ask about **specific events**, never generalities
- Follow **emotional** energy — when they get animated, dig deeper
- Never ask "Why?" directly — ask "Tell me more about that"
- Record and transcribe — nuance lives in exact words
- 8-12 interviews typically reveal the pattern

## Sub-Commands

### `/think:jtbd` — Full JTBD Analysis
Complete analysis covering all dimensions:
1. Identify the core job(s)
2. Map functional, emotional, and social dimensions
3. Build the universal job map
4. Identify opportunities using ODI scoring
5. Map the competitive landscape through jobs lens
6. Recommend strategic actions

### `/think:jtbd:map` — Job Mapping
Focused on the 8-step universal job map:
1. Walk through each step for the specified job
2. Identify pain points and friction at each step
3. Score opportunity at each step
4. Recommend where to innovate

### `/think:jtbd:forces` — Forces of Progress Analysis
Focused on the four forces diagram:
1. Identify Push factors (current dissatisfaction)
2. Identify Pull factors (new solution attraction)
3. Identify Anxiety factors (switching fears)
4. Identify Habit factors (inertia/comfort)
5. Calculate force balance
6. Recommend strategies to tip the balance

### `/think:jtbd:switch` — Switching Analysis
Focused on the switch timeline:
1. Map the customer's switching journey
2. Identify trigger events
3. Analyze passive vs. active looking behavior
4. Identify decision criteria and deal-breakers
5. Post-switch satisfaction factors
6. Recommend how to accelerate switching

## When to Use JTBD

| Situation | JTBD Approach |
|-----------|--------------|
| New product/service design | Full `/think:jtbd` analysis |
| Existing product improvement | `/think:jtbd:map` to find underserved steps |
| Marketing message creation | `/think:jtbd:forces` to find Push/Pull language |
| Competitive positioning | `/think:jtbd:switch` to understand switching triggers |
| Customer segmentation | Group by jobs, not demographics |
| Pricing strategy | Price based on value of job done, not cost-plus |
| Sales enablement | Understand hiring criteria at each timeline stage |
