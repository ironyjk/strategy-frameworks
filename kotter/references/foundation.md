# Kotter's 8-Step Change Model — Foundation & Theory

## Origin & Creator

**John P. Kotter** is the Konosuke Matsushita Professor of Leadership, Emeritus, at Harvard Business School. His 8-step model was first introduced in a 1995 Harvard Business Review article ("Leading Change: Why Transformation Efforts Fail") and expanded into the bestselling book *Leading Change* (1996).

The model was updated in 2014 with *Accelerate: Building Strategic Agility for a Faster-Moving World*, introducing the Dual Operating System concept.

---

## Why 70% of Change Efforts Fail — 8 Common Errors

Kotter's research at Harvard Business School identified that **70% of organizational change initiatives fail**. The root causes trace to eight predictable errors:

| # | Error (실패 원인) | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Allowing too much complacency (현상 안주 허용) | No one feels the need to change |
| 2 | Failing to create a powerful guiding coalition (추진력 있는 연합 부재) | Change has no political or organizational muscle |
| 3 | Underestimating the power of vision (비전의 힘 과소평가) | Direction unclear, people confused or resistant |
| 4 | Under-communicating the vision (비전 소통 부족) | Even a good vision dies if people don't hear it repeatedly |
| 5 | Permitting obstacles to block the vision (장애물 방치) | Structural, systemic, or human barriers kill momentum |
| 6 | Failing to create short-term wins (단기 성과 미창출) | People lose faith — "this isn't working" |
| 7 | Declaring victory too soon (너무 이른 승리 선언) | Regression to old habits; change doesn't stick |
| 8 | Neglecting to anchor in culture (문화 정착 실패) | New behaviors evaporate when pressure is off |

> **Key insight**: These errors are sequential. Skipping early steps or rushing later ones produces the same result — failure.

## The 8 Steps — Detailed Theory

### Step 1: Create Urgency (위기감 조성)

**What to do:**
- Examine market and competitive realities for threats and opportunities
- Identify and discuss crises, potential crises, or major opportunities
- Make the status quo more dangerous than the unknown
- Aim for 75%+ of leadership genuinely convinced change is necessary

**Pitfalls:**
- Paralysis from fear vs. productive urgency — urgency is NOT anxiety
- Over-relying on data alone; stories and experiences create visceral urgency
- Confusing false urgency (busy, frantic activity) with true urgency (focused determination)

**Success indicators:**
- People voluntarily start conversations about the need to change
- Meetings shift from "should we change?" to "how do we change?"
- Complacency-reinforcing behaviors (e.g., denying problems) decrease visibly

---

### Step 2: Build Guiding Coalition (변화추진팀 구성)

**What to do:**
- Assemble a group with enough power, expertise, credibility, and leadership to drive change
- Include people from different levels and departments — not just the C-suite
- Build trust within the coalition through off-site events, honest dialogue, and shared purpose
- Ensure the coalition operates outside the normal hierarchy

**Pitfalls:**
- Excluding key influencers (especially informal leaders)
- Coalition too homogeneous — lacks diverse perspectives
- Relying on authority alone instead of trust and shared commitment
- CEO tries to lead alone without a real coalition

**Success indicators:**
- Coalition members actively champion the change in their own areas
- The group can make decisions and act without constant executive approval
- Skeptics and resisters are engaged, not excluded
- Coalition has emotional commitment, not just intellectual agreement

---

### Step 3: Form Strategic Vision (전략적 비전 수립)

**What to do:**
- Create a clear, compelling vision of the future state
- Develop strategies to achieve that vision
- The vision must be: imaginable, desirable, feasible, focused, flexible, communicable
- Test: can you explain the vision in 5 minutes and get understanding AND interest?

**Pitfalls:**
- Vision too vague ("be the best") or too complex (50-page document)
- Confusing a vision with a plan — vision is the destination, strategy is the route
- Vision created by consultants, not owned by the coalition
- Vision doesn't connect emotionally; only appeals to logic

**Success indicators:**
- Anyone in the organization can articulate the vision in their own words
- People can see how their daily work connects to the vision
- The vision creates genuine excitement, not just compliance
- Strategies are clear enough to guide decision-making at all levels

---

### Step 4: Enlist Volunteer Army (자발적 참여 유도)

**What to do:**
- Communicate the vision relentlessly — 10x more than you think necessary
- Use every channel: meetings, memos, hallway conversations, town halls
- Lead by example — leadership behavior must embody the vision
- Address concerns and anxiety honestly; two-way communication, not broadcast
- The goal is a large-scale, voluntary movement — not a mandate

**Pitfalls:**
- Under-communicating by a factor of 10 (or 100)
- Saying one thing but doing another — kills credibility instantly
- Relying on a single announcement or email
- Ignoring or dismissing questions and pushback

**Success indicators:**
- People explain the change vision to their peers (viral spread)
- Voluntary advocates emerge at every level
- Resistance is acknowledged and addressed, not suppressed
- Leadership walk-the-talk is visible and consistent

---

### Step 5: Enable Action by Removing Barriers (장애물 제거)

**What to do:**
- Remove structural barriers: org charts, job descriptions, compensation systems
- Address misaligned systems: performance reviews, information systems
- Confront managers who undermine the change — the biggest barrier is often people
- Provide training and resources needed to act on the vision
- Empower broad-based action — let people experiment and take risks

**Pitfalls:**
- Trying to remove every barrier at once instead of prioritizing
- Ignoring the "boss barrier" — a powerful resistor who blocks their whole area
- Changing structures without changing people's skills
- Risk-averse culture punishes experiments and mistakes

**Success indicators:**
- People feel empowered to try new approaches without asking permission
- Structural and systemic obstacles are visibly being removed
- Change resisters are either converted or moved to non-blocking roles
- Training and resources are available and used

---

### Step 6: Generate Short-term Wins (단기 성과 창출)

**What to do:**
- Plan for visible, unambiguous performance improvements within 6-18 months
- Create those wins — don't just hope for them; engineer them deliberately
- Recognize and reward people who made the wins possible
- Use wins to validate the vision and strategies
- Build momentum and undermine cynics

**Pitfalls:**
- Leaving short-term wins to chance
- Wins that are ambiguous — skeptics can explain them away
- Not connecting wins to the change effort explicitly
- Too few wins or wins that only leadership sees

**Success indicators:**
- Multiple, visible, clearly change-related wins within 6-18 months
- Cynics and fence-sitters start joining the effort
- The wins prove the vision is achievable and worth the effort
- Each win builds confidence for the next, bigger challenge

---

### Step 7: Sustain Acceleration (가속화 유지)

**What to do:**
- Use credibility from early wins to tackle bigger, harder changes
- Don't let up — keep the urgency high even after early wins
- Launch new change projects, bring in additional people, expand scope
- Senior leadership stays focused on clarity and momentum
- Continuously identify and remove barriers as they emerge

**Pitfalls:**
- Declaring victory after the first wins (the #1 mistake at this stage)
- Letting urgency drop as "change fatigue" sets in
- Failing to tackle the deeper, systemic issues that require more effort
- Over-relying on the original coalition without bringing in new energy

**Success indicators:**
- More and harder changes are being tackled, not fewer
- Urgency remains high; the organization hasn't relaxed
- New leaders emerge to drive change in additional areas
- Interdependencies between change projects are managed

---

### Step 8: Institute Change (변화의 제도화)

**What to do:**
- Articulate the connection between new behaviors and organizational success
- Embed changes into: hiring, onboarding, promotion, performance systems
- Ensure leadership succession reinforces the change
- Create new cultural norms and shared values
- The change is "done" only when it's "how we do things here"

**Pitfalls:**
- Assuming culture changes automatically after behavior changes
- Neglecting to update HR systems, processes, and rituals
- Leadership succession puts someone in charge who doesn't embody the change
- Removing the supporting infrastructure too soon

**Success indicators:**
- New employees learn the new way as "normal" — they never knew the old way
- The change persists without the original champion pushing it
- Promotion and hiring criteria reflect the new culture
- People can't imagine going back to the old way

## Accelerate (2014 Update): Dual Operating System

In his 2014 book *Accelerate*, Kotter evolved the model to address the speed of modern change:

```
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │              DUAL OPERATING SYSTEM                       │
  │                                                          │
  │   HIERARCHY (관리 체계)          NETWORK (혁신 네트워크)    │
  │   ┌──────────────┐            ┌──────────────┐          │
  │   │  Reliability  │    ◀══▶   │  Agility      │          │
  │   │  Efficiency   │           │  Speed        │          │
  │   │  Optimization │           │  Innovation   │          │
  │   │  Structure    │           │  Opportunity  │          │
  │   └──────────────┘            └──────────────┘          │
  │                                                          │
  │   Runs the business             Changes the business     │
  │   (일상 운영)                    (변화 추진)               │
  │                                                          │
  │   Key: Both systems work TOGETHER, not in opposition     │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**5 Principles of the Accelerate model:**

1. **Many change agents, not just the usual few** — the network draws volunteers from across the hierarchy
2. **A want-to, not a have-to mentality** — people join voluntarily because they see the opportunity
3. **Head AND heart, not just head** — urgency plus emotional commitment
4. **Much more leadership, not just more management** — the network side is leadership-driven
5. **Two systems, one organization** — the hierarchy and network are inseparable, not a replacement

**When to use the Dual Operating System:**
- Change is continuous, not a one-time event
- The environment shifts faster than the hierarchy can adapt
- You need innovation AND operational excellence simultaneously
- Traditional project management approaches are too slow

## Comparison with Other Change Models

### Kotter vs. Lewin vs. ADKAR

| Dimension | Kotter (8 Steps) | Lewin (3 Stages) | ADKAR (Prosci) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Leadership-driven transformation | Force-field dynamics | Individual change journey |
| **Unit of change** | Organization | Group/System | Individual person |
| **Phases** | 8 sequential steps | Unfreeze → Change → Refreeze | Awareness → Desire → Knowledge → Ability → Reinforcement |
| **Strengths** | Actionable, addresses politics, urgency focus | Simple, intuitive, reveals barriers | Measurable per-person progress, gap analysis |
| **Weaknesses** | Linear assumption, less individual focus | Oversimplified, "refreeze" feels rigid | Doesn't address power, politics, coalition |
| **Best for** | Large-scale org transformation | Understanding resistance | Training rollouts, tech adoption |
| **Typical duration** | 12-36 months | Varies | 3-18 months per initiative |
| **Korean summary** | 리더십 중심 대규모 변혁 | 힘의 균형 분석 | 개인별 변화 여정 |

### When to Use Which

- **Use Kotter when:** The change is strategic, large-scale, requires coalition-building, involves culture shift
- **Use Lewin when:** You need to analyze forces for/against change, understand resistance, simple framing for teams
- **Use ADKAR when:** You need to track individual adoption, the change is more tactical (new system, new process), or you need to identify exactly where individuals are stuck
- **Combine them:** Kotter for overall strategy + ADKAR for individual tracking = powerful combination
