# Porter's Five Forces — Detailed Analysis Questions

Use these questions systematically when conducting a Five Forces analysis. For each force, rate as Low / Medium / High and provide evidence.

---

## Force 1: Competitive Rivalry (Industry Competition)

### Key Questions
1. How many competitors exist? Are they roughly equal in size, or is one dominant?
2. What is the industry growth rate? (Slow growth = more intense rivalry for share)
3. Are fixed costs high relative to variable costs? (High fixed costs drive price wars)
4. How differentiated are the products? (Commodity = intense rivalry)
5. What are the exit barriers? (High exit barriers trap firms in unprofitable competition)
6. How much excess capacity exists in the industry?
7. Is competition primarily on price, features, service, or brand?
8. How diverse are competitors' strategies and origins?
9. What is the frequency and impact of competitive moves?
10. Are there informational complexities (can firms monitor each other's actions)?

### Indicators of HIGH Rivalry
- Many competitors of similar size
- Slow industry growth or decline
- High fixed costs or perishable products
- Low differentiation / low switching costs
- High exit barriers (specialized assets, emotional attachment, government restrictions)
- High strategic stakes (prestige market, must-win territory)

### Indicators of LOW Rivalry
- Clear market leader with significant share advantage
- Fast industry growth (enough room for all)
- Differentiated products with loyal customer bases
- Low fixed costs, easy to scale up/down
- Low exit barriers

## Force 2: Threat of New Entrants

### Key Questions
1. What are the economies of scale? Do incumbents have significant cost advantages?
2. How much capital is required to enter? (Physical plant, R&D, brand building, working capital)
3. Can new entrants access distribution channels?
4. Are there proprietary technologies, patents, or trade secrets?
5. Do incumbents have experience curve advantages?
6. What is government policy? (Licensing, regulation, subsidies to incumbents)
7. How strong is brand loyalty? What are switching costs?
8. Will incumbents retaliate aggressively against new entrants?
9. Are there network effects that protect incumbents?
10. Can new entrants use new technology/business models to leapfrog barriers?

## Force 3: Bargaining Power of Suppliers

### Key Questions
1. How concentrated is the supplier base? (Few suppliers = high power)
2. Are there credible substitute inputs?
3. How important is the industry to the supplier? (Small customer = less leverage)
4. How important is the supplier's input to the buyer? (Critical input = high power)
5. How high are switching costs for changing suppliers?
6. Is the supplier's product differentiated?
7. Can the supplier credibly threaten forward integration?
8. Do suppliers have access to information about buyer margins?
9. Is the workforce unionized (labor as supplier)?
10. How much of the buyer's cost does this input represent?

### Supplier Power Matrix
| Factor | Low Power | High Power |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Supplier concentration | Fragmented | Concentrated |
| Substitute inputs | Many alternatives | Few/none |
| Differentiation | Commodity input | Unique input |
| Switching costs | Low | High |
| Forward integration | Unlikely | Credible threat |

## Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers

### Key Questions
1. How concentrated are buyers? Do a few buyers represent large volume?
2. Is the product a significant portion of the buyer's costs?
3. How standardized/undifferentiated is the product?
4. What are the buyer's switching costs?
5. How profitable are the buyers? (Low margins = more price pressure)
6. Can buyers credibly threaten backward integration?
7. How important is quality/performance relative to price?
8. How much information do buyers have about alternatives and costs?
9. Who makes the buying decision? (Procurement vs. end-user vs. influencer)
10. Can buyers play competitors against each other?

### B2B vs B2C Considerations
- **B2B**: Concentrated buyers, professional purchasing, explicit cost pressure
- **B2C**: Fragmented buyers, brand influence, emotional factors
- **Distributors/Retailers**: Intermediate buyers who may exercise significant power (Walmart effect)

## Force 5: Threat of Substitutes

### Key Questions
1. What products/services from other industries could fulfill the same need?
2. What is the price-performance ratio of substitutes vs. the industry's product?
3. What are the switching costs for buyers moving to substitutes?
4. How propensity-to-substitute varies by buyer segment?
5. Are substitute technologies improving faster than the industry's technology?
6. Are there trends (regulatory, social, technological) favoring substitutes?
7. Is the substitute from a high-profit industry (able to subsidize entry)?
8. Can the substitute be combined with other products to increase attractiveness?
9. How visible and accessible are substitutes to buyers?
10. What is the psychological switching cost (habit, familiarity)?

### Substitute Identification Framework
Think about the **function** your product performs, not the **form**:
- What job does the customer hire your product to do?
- What other products/services could do that same job?
- What does the customer do as an alternative (including "do nothing")?

Examples:
| Industry | Obvious Substitute | Non-obvious Substitute |
|----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Airlines (short-haul) | Train, car | Video conferencing |
| Movie theaters | Streaming | Social media, gaming |
| Bottled water | Tap water | Water filters |
| Traditional university | Online courses | Bootcamps, apprenticeships |

## Complementors (2008 Update — Quasi-Sixth Force)

### Key Questions
1. What products/services are used together with ours?
2. Are complement markets healthy and growing?
3. How do complementors affect buyer willingness to pay?
4. Can complementors increase or decrease barriers to entry?
5. Do complementors give buyers more or less power?
6. Could a complementor become a competitor (vertical integration)?

### Analysis Approach
Do not treat complementors as a separate force. Instead, assess how they modify each of the five forces:
- Do complements **raise entry barriers** (need ecosystem) or **lower them** (platform effect)?
- Do complements **increase buyer power** (more choices) or **decrease it** (lock-in)?
- Do complements **create substitutes** or **defend against them**?
