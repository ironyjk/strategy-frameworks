# Porter's Five Forces — Industry Example Analyses

Three complete example analyses to illustrate how the framework applies in practice.

---

## Example 1: Commercial Airlines (2024)

### Rivalry: HIGH
- Major carriers (Delta, United, American, Southwest in US; Ryanair, Lufthansa, Emirates globally)
- Price-sensitive market with transparent pricing (aggregators make comparison instant)
- High fixed costs (aircraft, gates, routes) drive volume competition
- Cyclical demand; overcapacity during downturns
- Product is largely undifferentiated in economy class
- High exit barriers (union contracts, specialized assets, government involvement)

### Threat of New Entrants: LOW-MEDIUM
- Massive capital requirements (aircraft, gates, maintenance infrastructure)
- Regulatory barriers (routes, slots, safety certification)
- Brand loyalty through frequent flyer programs
- BUT: low-cost carriers (Spirit, Frontier) continue to enter niche routes
- Leasing models reduce capital barrier somewhat

### Supplier Power: HIGH
- Aircraft duopoly (Boeing/Airbus) for large commercial planes
- Engine manufacturers (GE, Rolls-Royce, Pratt & Whitney) — few alternatives
- Airport slots are scarce at major hubs
- Oil suppliers (partially hedged, but still major cost)
- Unionized labor (pilots, mechanics) has significant bargaining power
- IT systems (Amadeus, Sabre) concentrated

### Buyer Power: HIGH
- Price transparency through aggregators (Google Flights, Kayak, Skyscanner)
- Low switching costs for leisure travelers
- Corporate buyers can negotiate volume discounts
- Frequent flyer programs create some lock-in (reduces buyer power slightly)
- Individual buyer purchases are small but collectively decisive

### Threat of Substitutes: MEDIUM
- Short-haul: High-speed rail (Europe, Asia), car, bus
- Long-haul: Very few practical substitutes
- Video conferencing is a real substitute for business travel (proven during COVID)
- Remote work reduces business travel demand structurally

### Overall Assessment
**Industry attractiveness: LOW.** Airlines are notoriously difficult to profit from. Warren Buffett called them "a death trap for investors" (before later investing in them, then divesting). The combination of high rivalry, powerful suppliers, powerful buyers, and high fixed costs makes sustained profitability challenging. Winners differentiate through operational efficiency (Southwest), premium service (Singapore Airlines), or hub dominance (Delta at ATL).

## Example 2: Cloud Computing / IaaS (2024)

### Rivalry: MEDIUM-HIGH
- Three dominant players: AWS (~31%), Azure (~25%), GCP (~11%)
- Smaller players: Oracle, IBM, Alibaba, regional providers
- Price competition is real but slowing (focus shifting to services/AI)
- High fixed costs (data centers) but also massive scale advantages
- Growing market reduces zero-sum competition
- Differentiation through ecosystem/services more than raw compute

### Threat of New Entrants: LOW
- Enormous capital requirements ($10B+ annual capex for major players)
- Massive economies of scale (network, data center, purchasing power)
- Ecosystem lock-in (proprietary services, APIs, tooling)
- Talent requirements (specialized engineering at scale)
- Customer migration costs create switching barriers
- BUT: Niche players can compete in specific verticals or regions

### Supplier Power: LOW-MEDIUM
- Custom hardware design (hyperscalers design their own chips increasingly)
- Server commoditization reduces traditional supplier power
- BUT: NVIDIA has near-monopoly on AI training GPUs (very high power in AI segment)
- Real estate for data centers becoming scarce in key markets
- Power/energy increasingly important and sometimes scarce

### Buyer Power: MEDIUM
- Small-medium businesses: low power individually, moderate collectively
- Enterprise: significant power (multi-million dollar contracts, multi-cloud leverage)
- Switching costs are high once workloads are deployed (reduces buyer power)
- Multi-cloud strategies increasing buyer leverage
- Open-source/containerization (Kubernetes) reduces lock-in somewhat

### Threat of Substitutes: LOW
- On-premises infrastructure is a "reverse substitute" — declining but not dead
- Edge computing complements rather than substitutes cloud
- Hybrid cloud is the norm, not a true substitute
- For most workloads, cloud has no practical alternative at comparable cost/scale
- Sovereign/government clouds: regulatory driven, not cost driven

### Overall Assessment
**Industry attractiveness: HIGH** for incumbents, **LOW** for new entrants. The cloud market is large ($600B+), growing (15-20% annually), and consolidating around 3 players. Margins are strong (AWS 30%+ operating margin). However, AI is reshaping competitive dynamics — whoever provides the best AI infrastructure platform may capture disproportionate growth. The biggest risk is concentration of power in GPU supply (NVIDIA).

## Example 3: Industrial Cleaning Services (South Korea) — DY Industry Context

### Rivalry: MEDIUM-HIGH
- Many small-to-medium competitors in regional markets
- Low product differentiation in standard cleaning services
- Price competition is common for commodity work (basic tank cleaning)
- Relationships and track record matter for large contract customers (chemical plants, refineries)
- Seasonal and cyclical demand (turnaround seasons, maintenance windows)
- Some regional concentration (Ulsan industrial complex, Yeosu, Daesan)

### Threat of New Entrants: MEDIUM
- Moderate capital requirements (specialized vehicles, equipment)
- Certifications and safety qualifications required (industrial safety, environmental permits)
- Relationship-based sales create soft barriers
- Specialized equipment (vacuum trucks, high-pressure units) requires investment
- BUT: labor-intensive business with many small operators who can enter specific service lines
- Low technology barriers for basic services

### Supplier Power: LOW-MEDIUM
- Equipment suppliers are diverse (vehicles, cleaning chemicals, safety gear)
- Labor is the primary input — labor market tightness affects power
- Subcontractors (daily workers) are available but quality varies
- No single supplier dominates
- Specialized equipment (dredging vehicles) has fewer suppliers

### Buyer Power: HIGH
- Large industrial customers (petrochemical, refinery) have significant negotiating power
- Long-term maintenance contracts awarded through bidding processes
- Customers can and do switch providers
- Price transparency in bidding (reverse auctions common)
- Concentration risk: losing a top customer (e.g., SK Hynix at 30%+ of revenue) is devastating
- BUT: switching costs increase with safety qualification/plant knowledge

### Threat of Substitutes: LOW
- Industrial plants must be cleaned — regulatory requirement
- Automated cleaning solutions exist for some applications but are complementary, not substitutes
- In-house cleaning teams at large plants are a partial substitute
- Environmental regulations are increasing, which increases demand
- Some substitution between cleaning methods (chemical vs. mechanical vs. hydro)

### Overall Assessment
**Industry attractiveness: MEDIUM.** Profitable but competitive. Key success factors:
1. **Customer concentration management** — diversify beyond top 3 customers
2. **Specialization premium** — move from commodity cleaning to specialized services (heat exchanger, environmental remediation) where margins are higher
3. **Equipment as moat** — specialized equipment (dredging vehicles) creates competitive advantage
4. **Safety record** — zero incidents enables access to premium customers
5. **Geographic density** — cluster operations near industrial complexes to reduce transit costs

Strategic positioning for DY: Focus differentiation — specialize in high-value services (tank cleaning, sludge dehydration, heat exchanger maintenance) rather than competing on price in basic industrial cleaning. Equipment investment (dredging vehicles) and safety excellence are the primary differentiators.
