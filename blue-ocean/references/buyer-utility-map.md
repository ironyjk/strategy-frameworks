# Blue Ocean Strategy — Buyer Utility Map (Complete Reference)

The Buyer Utility Map is a diagnostic tool to identify where your industry focuses — and where untapped utility spaces exist for blue ocean creation.

---

## The 6 Stages of the Buyer Experience Cycle

### 1. Purchase
The entire experience of finding and buying the product/service.
- How long does it take to find the product?
- Is the purchase environment attractive and accessible?
- How quickly can you complete the transaction?
- Key questions: Is the buying process transparent? Easy? Enjoyable?

### 2. Delivery
Getting the product from purchase point to where it will be used.
- How long does delivery take?
- How difficult is it to unpack and install?
- Do buyers need to arrange delivery themselves?
- Key questions: Is delivery stressful? Time-consuming? Unreliable?

### 3. Use
The core experience of using the product/service.
- Does it require training or expert assistance?
- Is it easy to store when not in use?
- How effective are its features and functions?
- Key questions: Does it do what buyers really need? Is it intuitive?

### 4. Supplements
Other products/services required to make it work.
- What else do buyers need? (Accessories, consumables, add-ons)
- How expensive and hard to obtain are supplements?
- How much time do supplements consume?
- Key questions: Are supplements a hidden cost? A pain point?

### 5. Maintenance
Keeping the product in working order over its lifetime.
- Does it require external maintenance or upgrades?
- How easy is it to maintain?
- How expensive is ongoing maintenance?
- Key questions: Is maintenance a burden? Can it be eliminated?

### 6. Disposal
What happens at end of life.
- Does the product create waste?
- Are there legal/environmental disposal requirements?
- How difficult and costly is disposal?
- Key questions: Does disposal create anxiety? Cost? Environmental guilt?

## The 6 Utility Levers

### 1. Customer Productivity
Can you help customers do things faster, better, or with less effort?
- Reduce time wasted
- Reduce steps required
- Increase output per unit of input

### 2. Simplicity
Can you eliminate complexity?
- Remove jargon, technical requirements, expertise needed
- Streamline processes
- Make the offering intuitive and straightforward

### 3. Convenience
Can you make it available when and where customers want it?
- 24/7 availability
- No special trips or arrangements needed
- Works across contexts/situations

### 4. Risk Reduction
Can you reduce financial, physical, or emotional risks?
- Guarantees, warranties
- Safety improvements
- Reputation/social risk reduction

### 5. Fun and Image
Can you provide emotional value?
- Aesthetic pleasure
- Status / identity expression
- Enjoyment of the experience itself

### 6. Environmental Friendliness
Can you reduce environmental impact?
- Sustainable materials
- Reduced waste / emissions
- Circular economy alignment
- Regulatory compliance made easy

## How to Use the Buyer Utility Map

### Step 1: Map Your Industry
For the industry as a whole (not just your company), mark each cell:
- Where does the industry focus most of its competition?
- Where does the industry completely ignore utility?

### Step 2: Identify Blue Ocean Spaces
- Empty cells = potential blue ocean opportunities
- Especially powerful: cells where buyers experience significant pain that no one addresses

### Step 3: Validate with Noncustomers
- Ask: "Why don't you use this industry's products?"
- Their answers often point to specific utility gaps on the map

### Step 4: Connect to ERRC
- Empty cells with high pain → **Create** (new factors)
- Overcrowded cells → **Reduce** or **Eliminate**
- The new value curve should address the empty cells

## Classic Examples

### Example: Cirque du Soleil
Traditional circus industry competed heavily on:
- Use + Fun/Image (animal acts, star performers)
- Purchase + Convenience (tent, traveling show)

Cirque du Soleil found blue ocean in:
- Use + Fun/Image (artistic theater experience)
- Purchase + Risk Reduction (comfortable venue, premium positioning)
- Eliminated: Animals, star performers, multiple rings (reduced cost)

### Example: Dyson Vacuum
Traditional vacuum industry:
- Use + Productivity (suction power)
- Maintenance + Convenience (bag replacement)

Dyson's blue ocean:
- Maintenance + Simplicity (no bags ever)
- Use + Productivity (no suction loss)
- Disposal + Environmental Friendliness (no bags in landfill)

### Example: Netflix (vs. Blockbuster)
Blockbuster competed on:
- Purchase + Convenience (many store locations)
- Use + Fun/Image (browsing physical shelves)

Netflix (DVD-by-mail, then streaming) found:
- Purchase + Convenience (no trip to store, no late fees)
- Purchase + Risk Reduction (flat monthly fee, no late fees)
- Disposal + Simplicity (mail it back, or streaming = nothing to return)
- Use + Productivity (algorithm recommendations save browsing time)
