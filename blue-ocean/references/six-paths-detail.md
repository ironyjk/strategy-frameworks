# Blue Ocean Strategy — Six Paths Framework (Complete Reference)

The Six Paths Framework provides six systematic approaches to reconstruct market boundaries and find blue ocean opportunities. Instead of looking within the accepted boundaries of competition, you look across them.

---

## Path 1: Look Across Alternative Industries

### Principle
Competitors are not just direct rivals. Alternatives are products/services with different forms but the same function or purpose.

### Key Questions
- What alternatives do customers choose between when making a decision?
- Why do customers trade across alternatives?
- What are the key factors that cause buyers to trade up or down between alternatives?

### How to Apply
1. List the alternative industries (not just substitute products)
2. Understand why buyers switch between them
3. Identify what your industry could learn from the alternative

### Examples
- **Southwest Airlines** looked across airlines AND car travel. Created short-haul, low-cost, frequent flights that competed with driving.
- **NTT DoCoMo i-mode** looked across mobile phones AND the internet. Created mobile internet before smartphones.
- **Home Depot** looked across professional contractors AND hardware stores. Created the DIY superstore.

### Template
```
Our industry: _______________
Alternative industries: 
1. _______________ (why buyers choose this instead)
2. _______________ (why buyers choose this instead)
3. _______________ (why buyers choose this instead)

Key insight: What would happen if we combined the best of ___ and ___?
```

## Path 2: Look Across Strategic Groups Within the Industry

### Principle
Most industries have strategic groups — clusters of companies pursuing similar strategies (luxury vs. economy, specialist vs. generalist). Blue oceans exist in the gaps between groups.

### Key Questions
- What are the strategic groups in your industry?
- Why do buyers trade up to a higher group?
- Why do buyers trade down to a lower group?
- What if you offered the key advantage of the upper group at the price point of the lower?

### How to Apply
1. Map the strategic groups on two axes (usually price and some performance dimension)
2. Identify what makes buyers move between groups
3. Design an offering that captures the decisive advantages of two groups while eliminating the rest

### Examples
- **Toyota Lexus** looked across luxury cars (Mercedes) and economy cars (Toyota). Created affordable luxury.
- **Curves fitness** looked across full-service gyms and home exercise programs. Created simple, affordable, women-only circuit training.
- **Ralph Lauren** looked across haute couture and mass fashion. Created "masstige" — mass prestige.

### Template
```
Strategic groups in our industry:
Group A (high-end): Key advantage = _______________
Group B (low-end): Key advantage = _______________
Group C (mid-market): Key advantage = _______________

Why do buyers trade UP from B to A? _______________
Why do buyers trade DOWN from A to B? _______________

Blue ocean opportunity: Offer [Group A advantage] at [Group B price/simplicity]
```

## Path 3: Look Across the Chain of Buyers

### Principle
There are usually three types of "buyers": purchasers (who pay), users (who use), and influencers (who affect the decision). Most industries converge on one. Shifting focus to a different buyer in the chain can reveal blue ocean.

### Key Questions
- Who are the purchasers, users, and influencers in your industry?
- Which one does the industry primarily target?
- What would happen if you shifted focus to a different buyer group?

### How to Apply
1. Map the buyer chain: purchaser, user, influencer
2. Identify which group the industry over-indexes on
3. Understand the needs of the neglected buyer group
4. Redesign the offering for that group

### Examples
- **Bloomberg terminal** shifted from IT purchasers to trader users. Made terminals user-friendly for traders, not IT departments.
- **Novo Nordisk insulin** shifted from doctors (influencers) to patients (users). Created easy-to-use insulin pens.
- **Canon copiers** shifted from corporate purchasers to individual users. Created personal desktop copiers.

### Template
```
Buyer chain:
- Purchaser (pays): _______________ 
- User (uses): _______________
- Influencer (recommends): _______________

Industry focuses on: _______________
Neglected buyer: _______________
What does the neglected buyer actually need? _______________
```

## Path 4: Look Across Complementary Products and Services

### Principle
Products and services are rarely used in isolation. There is untapped value in addressing what happens before, during, and after the use of your product.

### Key Questions
- What context is your product used in?
- What happens before, during, and after people use your product?
- What are the biggest pain points in the total solution (including complements)?
- Can you solve the complement problem and incorporate it into your offering?

### How to Apply
1. Map the total buyer experience (not just your product)
2. Identify pain points in complementary products/services
3. Consider bundling or integrating to solve the total problem

### Examples
- **NABI (bus manufacturer)** looked beyond the bus to total lifecycle cost. Used fiberglass (vs. steel) — higher purchase price but dramatically lower maintenance and longer life. Sold to municipalities on total cost of ownership.
- **Philips** tea kettle with built-in water filter addressed the complementary problem of limescale in hard-water regions.
- **Dyson** Airblade hand dryer eliminated the complement of paper towels entirely.

### Template
```
Our product: _______________

Before use: _______________ (pain points: _______________)
During use: _______________ (pain points: _______________)
After use: _______________ (pain points: _______________)
Complements needed: _______________ (pain points: _______________)

Biggest pain in the total experience: _______________
Could we solve this and make it part of our offering? _______________
```

## Path 5: Look Across Functional or Emotional Appeal

### Principle
Some industries compete on rational/functional appeal (price, utility, features). Others compete on emotional appeal (feelings, image, experience). Switching orientation can unlock blue ocean.

### Key Questions
- Does your industry compete primarily on function or emotion?
- If functional: What emotional elements could be added?
- If emotional: What could be stripped to functional basics at lower cost?

### How to Apply
1. Determine whether your industry is functionally or emotionally oriented
2. Challenge that orientation
3. For emotional industries: strip away emotional elements, reduce price, offer functional basics
4. For functional industries: add emotional elements that create new demand

### Examples
- **Swatch** transformed watches from functional timekeeping (commodity) to emotional fashion accessories. Same basic function, completely different value proposition.
- **The Body Shop** stripped emotional beauty marketing (supermodels, fantasy) and offered functional, natural products with social responsibility.
- **QB House (Japan)** stripped emotional salon experience (massage, shampoo, coffee) to 10-minute, $10 functional haircut. Eliminated 80% of the experience; 10x faster.
- **Cemex** transformed cement delivery from functional commodity to emotional reliability promise ("delivery in 20 minutes or it's free" — like Domino's for cement).

### Template
```
Industry orientation: Functional / Emotional

If FUNCTIONAL → What emotions are we missing?
- Status/image: _______________
- Community/belonging: _______________
- Joy/delight: _______________
- Trust/security: _______________

If EMOTIONAL → What functional basics are overpriced?
- Core function that matters: _______________
- Emotional fluff that could be cut: _______________
- Price reduction possible: _______________
```

## Path 6: Look Across Time (Trends)

### Principle
All industries are affected by external trends. Instead of adapting incrementally, look at how a trend will change value to buyers and project forward.

### Key Questions
- What trends have a high probability of impacting your industry?
- Are these trends irreversible?
- Do they have a clear trajectory?
- How will these trends change what buyers value?

### How to Apply
1. Identify trends that are decisive (not marginal), irreversible, and have a clear trajectory
2. Ask: "If this trend reaches its logical conclusion, what would the market look like?"
3. Work backward from that future to design today's offering

### Key Trend Categories
- **Technology**: AI, automation, IoT, blockchain, biotech
- **Regulation**: Environmental, privacy, safety, trade
- **Demographics**: Aging population, urbanization, workforce shifts
- **Social**: Remote work, sustainability consciousness, health awareness
- **Economic**: Wealth distribution, emerging middle classes, cost structures

### Examples
- **Apple iTunes** looked at the trend of music piracy (Napster) and asked: "Where is this heading?" Created legal, easy, per-song digital music before the industry adapted.
- **Tesla** looked at converging trends: environmental regulation tightening, battery technology improving, gas prices volatile. Built the car for 2020+ in 2010.
- **Airbnb** looked at: sharing economy trend + smartphone ubiquity + recession frugality + desire for authentic travel experiences.

### Template
```
Key trends affecting our industry:
1. _______________ (irreversible? clear trajectory?)
2. _______________ (irreversible? clear trajectory?)
3. _______________ (irreversible? clear trajectory?)

If trend #___ reaches its logical conclusion in 5 years:
- What will buyers value? _______________
- What will buyers NOT value? _______________
- What new demand will exist? _______________

What offering would win in that future? _______________
Can we build it now? _______________
```

## Combining the Six Paths

The most powerful blue ocean strategies often combine insights from multiple paths.

| Path | Question Essence |
|------|-----------------|
| 1. Alternative Industries | What else could they buy instead? |
| 2. Strategic Groups | What if high-end quality met low-end price? |
| 3. Buyer Chain | Who are we NOT selling to? |
| 4. Complements | What pain exists around our product? |
| 5. Function vs. Emotion | Are we selling utility or feelings? Switch it. |
| 6. Time/Trends | What will they want in 3-5 years? |

### Process
1. Explore all six paths individually
2. Identify the 2-3 most promising insights
3. Combine into an ERRC Grid
4. Draw the new Strategy Canvas
5. Validate with the Buyer Utility Map and BOI Index
