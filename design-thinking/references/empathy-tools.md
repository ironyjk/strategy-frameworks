# Empathy Tools — Understanding Users Deeply

## Why Empathy First?

Design Thinking begins with empathy because the biggest risk in innovation is solving the wrong problem. Most product failures are not engineering failures — they are empathy failures. The team built what THEY thought was needed, not what USERS actually needed.

### How to Fill It

1. Start with ONE specific person (not a demographic segment)
2. Use real quotes and observed behaviors, not assumptions
3. Pay attention to contradictions (says one thing, does another)
4. The PAIN/GAIN section drives the Define phase

### When to Use
- Before designing anything
- When the team disagrees about user needs
- When data tells you WHAT but not WHY
- When entering a new market or user segment

## Tool 2: User Interview Guide

### The Principles

1. **Ask about behavior, not preferences**: "Tell me about the last time you..." NOT "Would you like a feature that...?"
2. **Follow the energy**: When users get animated (excited or frustrated), dig deeper
3. **Silence is golden**: Pause after answers. Users fill silence with deeper insights.
4. **Ask "why?" five times**: Surface answers hide deeper motivations
5. **Capture quotes verbatim**: Paraphrasing loses nuance

### Interview Structure (45-60 minutes)

**Opening (5 min)**:
- "Thank you for your time. There are no right or wrong answers."
- "I'm here to learn from you, not to test anything."
- "Is it okay if I take notes / record?"

**Context (10 min)**:
- "Tell me about your role / typical day."
- "Walk me through how you currently handle [task/problem]."
- "What tools/methods do you use?"

**Deep Dive (25 min)**:
- "Tell me about the last time you [experienced the problem]."
- "What happened? Then what? Then what?" (follow the story)
- "What was the hardest part?"
- "What did you try? Did it work? Why/why not?"
- "How did that make you feel?"
- "If you could wave a magic wand, what would change?"

**Extremes and Edges (10 min)**:
- "What's the BEST experience you've had with [domain]?"
- "What's the WORST?"
- "Tell me about a workaround you've invented."
- "What do you wish someone understood about your situation?"

**Closing (5 min)**:
- "Is there anything I should have asked but didn't?"
- "Is there anyone else I should talk to?"
- "Can I follow up if I have more questions?"

### Questions to AVOID
| Bad Question | Why It's Bad | Better Version |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| "Would you use X?" | Hypothetical; people can't predict behavior | "How do you currently handle X?" |
| "Do you like X?" | Leading; social desirability bias | "Tell me about your experience with X." |
| "Don't you think X is a problem?" | Leading; suggests the answer | "What challenges do you face with X?" |
| "How much would you pay for X?" | Hypothetical pricing is unreliable | "What do you currently spend on solving this?" |
| "What features do you want?" | Users design solutions, not identify needs | "What frustrates you most about the current situation?" |

## Tool 3: Observation (Ethnographic)

### The Protocol

1. **Go to their environment**: Office, factory floor, home, commute — wherever the problem lives
2. **Watch, don't interact** (at first): Note what they do, in what order, with what tools
3. **Look for**:
   - **Workarounds**: Sticky notes, spreadsheets, manual processes → unmet needs
   - **Error recovery**: What happens when things go wrong?
   - **Collaboration**: Who do they ask for help? How?
   - **Sequence**: What is the actual workflow (not the documented one)?
   - **Environment**: Physical setup, distractions, tools within reach
4. **Note the gap between SAY and DO**: People's reported behavior differs from actual behavior

### Observation Framework: AEIOU

| Category | What to Observe |
|----------|----------------|
| **A**ctivities | What are they doing? Steps, sequences, timing |
| **E**nvironment | Where are they? Layout, tools, atmosphere |
| **I**nteractions | With whom/what? Conversations, tool usage |
| **O**bjects | What things do they use? Artifacts, tools, documents |
| **U**sers | Who? Roles, demographics, attitudes, behaviors |

```
           triggers    │  evaluating │  deciding   │  exploring  │  rations
───────────────────────┼─────────────┼─────────────┼─────────────┼──────────
FEELING:   😐 ──── 😊 ──── 😟 ──── 😊 ──── 😊 ──── 😟 ──── 😊 ──── 😡 ──

 curve)                                                                 
───────────────────────┼─────────────┼─────────────┼─────────────┼──────────

             of mouth  │  reviews    │  payment    │  email      │  center
───────────────────────┼─────────────┼─────────────┼─────────────┼──────────
PAIN POINTS: [specific pain at each phase]
OPPORTUNITIES: [what could improve at each phase]
```

### How to Build It

1. Choose ONE user persona
2. Define the phases (beginning to end of their journey)
3. Fill in from LEFT to RIGHT using interview/observation data
4. Plot the emotional curve — dips are opportunity zones
5. Identify the MOMENTS OF TRUTH (highest emotional stakes)

### Moments of Truth
- **First moment**: First impression (will they continue?)
- **Second moment**: First real use (does it deliver on the promise?)
- **Zero moment**: The research phase before they even reach you (ZMOT — Google's concept)

## Tool 5: "Five Whys" for Root Cause

### Method

Start with an observable problem. Ask "Why?" five times to reach the root cause.

**Example**:
1. "Users aren't completing the onboarding." — Why?
2. "They drop off at step 3 (connect account)." — Why?
3. "They don't have their API key readily available." — Why?
4. "The API key is buried in their settings page on another platform." — Why?
5. "No one told them they'd need it before starting onboarding."

**Root cause**: The pre-onboarding communication doesn't prepare users for what they'll need.

**Fix**: Send a "what to have ready" email before onboarding, not a redesign of step 3.

### Rules
- Each "why" must be answered with observable evidence, not speculation
- If you can't answer a "why," that's where you need more research
- Sometimes you need 3 whys, sometimes 7 — 5 is a guideline, not a rule
- Multiple branches are common (there may be several "whys" at each level)

## Tool 6: Persona Creation

### What a Good Persona Looks Like

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
PERSONA: [Name]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

DEMOGRAPHICS
Age: [X]  |  Role: [X]  |  Experience: [X years]

QUOTE (real, from interview)
"[Verbatim quote that captures their worldview]"

GOALS
1. [Primary goal — what they're trying to achieve]
2. [Secondary goal]
3. [Aspirational goal — what they dream of]

FRUSTRATIONS
1. [Primary pain point]
2. [Secondary pain point]
3. [Workaround they currently use]

BEHAVIORS
• [How they currently solve the problem]
• [Tools they use]
• [Habits and preferences]

CONTEXT
• [When/where they encounter the problem]
• [Who influences their decisions]
• [Constraints they face]

SCENARIO
"When [trigger], [Name] needs to [action] so that [outcome].
Currently, [Name] [current behavior], which causes [frustration]."
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

### Persona Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Problem | Fix |
|-------------|---------|-----|
| Demographic-only personas | "35-year-old female" tells you nothing about needs | Focus on behaviors and goals |
| Aspirational personas | Describes who you WISH your users were | Base on real research data |
| Too many personas | Can't design for 12 people | 3-5 primary personas maximum |
| "Average" persona | Averages mask important differences | Represent distinct behavior patterns |
| Static personas | Never updated | Review quarterly with new research |

## Tool 7: Stakeholder Map

### When There Are Multiple Users

Many products serve multiple stakeholders with different (sometimes conflicting) needs:

```
           HIGH INFLUENCE
                │
    ┌───────────┼───────────┐
    │  KEEP     │  MANAGE   │
    │ SATISFIED │  CLOSELY  │
    │           │           │
────┼───────────┼───────────┼────
    │           │           │
    │  MONITOR  │  KEEP     │
    │  (minimal │ INFORMED  │
    │   effort) │           │
    └───────────┼───────────┘
                │
           LOW INFLUENCE
    LOW INTEREST ─── HIGH INTEREST
```

### Example: Enterprise Software
- **Manage Closely** (high influence, high interest): Department head who approved purchase
- **Keep Satisfied** (high influence, low interest): CIO who sets IT policy
- **Keep Informed** (low influence, high interest): End users who use it daily
- **Monitor** (low influence, low interest): IT security team (until something goes wrong)

Design for the end users (high interest), but don't ignore the approvers (high influence).
