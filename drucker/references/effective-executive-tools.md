# The Effective Executive — Practical Tools

From Peter Drucker's *The Effective Executive* (1966, revised 2006)

## Overview

Drucker's core insight: effectiveness is a **habit** — a set of practices that can be learned. Intelligence, knowledge, and imagination are useless without effectiveness. These five practices are the tools.

---

## Practice 1: Know Where Your Time Goes

### The Problem
Most executives have no idea where their time actually goes. They believe they spend time on important matters. They don't.

### Tool: The Time Log

**Step 1: Record (2 weeks)**
Track every activity in 15-minute blocks. Don't rely on memory — record in real time.

```
Time Log Template:
| Time Block | Activity | Category | Value? |
|------------|----------|----------|--------|
| 08:00-08:15 | Email triage | Admin | Low |
| 08:15-08:45 | Customer call re: contract | Revenue | High |
| 08:45-09:00 | Walked to get coffee, chatted | Social | None |
| 09:00-10:00 | Staff meeting (weekly) | Meeting | Medium |
```

Categories: Revenue, Strategy, People, Admin, Meeting, Firefighting, Learning

**Step 2: Analyze**
After 2 weeks, ask three questions for every activity:
1. "What would happen if this were not done at all?" — If the answer is "nothing," stop doing it.
2. "Which of my activities could be done by somebody else just as well, or better?" — Delegate it.
3. "What do I do that wastes YOUR time without contributing to your effectiveness?" — Ask direct reports this question directly.

**Step 3: Consolidate**
- Block discretionary time into large chunks (minimum 90 minutes)
- Schedule "maker time" vs. "manager time"
- Protect the consolidated blocks ruthlessly

### Time Wasters to Eliminate
| Time Waster | Root Cause | Fix |
|-------------|-----------|-----|
| Recurring crises | Lack of system/process | Create a standard procedure so it becomes routine |
| Too many meetings | Unclear decision rights | Define who decides what; most meetings are information that could be a memo |
| Excess reports | Legacy requirements | Ask "Who reads this? What do they do with it?" |
| Overstaffing | Organization sprawl | If managers spend >10% of time on "people problems," the unit is too large |
| Malorganization | Work requiring multiple people that should require one | Reorganize to minimize handoffs |

## Practice 2: Focus on Contribution

### The Tool: The Contribution Question

Ask yourself regularly: **"What can I contribute that will significantly affect the performance and results of the institution I serve?"**

### Three Areas of Contribution
1. **Direct results** — revenue, profit, output (the "bottom line" of your role)
2. **Building and reaffirming values** — what does this organization stand for?
3. **Developing people** — including yourself (the organization of tomorrow)

### Practical Application: The Meeting Test
Before every meeting, ask: "Why are we having this meeting? What contribution should come out of it?"

After every meeting, document: "What did we decide? Who is responsible? By when?"

### Contribution Matrix
```
| My Role's Contribution | Status | Next Action |
|------------------------|--------|-------------|
| Direct results: [specific] | On/Off track | |
| Values: [what I model] | Strong/Weak | |
| People: [who I'm developing] | Active/Passive | |
```

## Practice 3: Build on Strengths

### The Tool: Strength Audit

**For yourself:**
1. When you make a key decision or take a key action, write down what you expect to happen
2. 9-12 months later, compare actual results with expectations
3. After 2-3 years, you will know: where your strengths lie, what you do that limits your strengths, what you cannot do at all

This is Drucker's "feedback analysis" — predating modern strengths assessments by decades.

**For staffing decisions:**
| Question | Wrong Answer | Right Answer |
|----------|-------------|--------------|
| "Can this person do the job?" | "They have no weaknesses" | "Their strengths fit the key demands of this position" |
| "What about their weaknesses?" | "We need to fix them" | "Are the weaknesses in areas that matter for THIS job?" |
| "What if they fail?" | "We chose wrong" | "We matched their strengths to the wrong task" |

### The Staffing Rule
"Whoever tries to place a person or staff an organization to avoid weakness will end up at best with mediocrity."

Staffing decision framework:
1. What is the one key task this position must accomplish?
2. Who among the candidates has the strength for that one task?
3. Is this strength relevant and proven (not hypothetical)?
4. Can I structure the role to make the weakness irrelevant?

## Practice 4: First Things First

### The Tool: The Posterity Decision

"The executive who wants to be effective and who wants the organization to be effective polishes the short list of priorities and insists on their supremacy."

**Priority Setting Rules (Drucker's rules, not Eisenhower's):**
1. Pick the future over the past
2. Focus on opportunity, not on problem
3. Choose your own direction — don't follow the crowd
4. Aim high — for something that will make a difference, not something "safe" and easy

**The "Systematic Abandonment" Exercise:**
Every 6 months, ask of every activity, product, process, market:
- "If we were not already doing this, would we start doing it now?"
- If the answer is no: "How can we get out — fast?"

### Priority vs. Posterity
- **Priority**: What to do FIRST (most people can manage this)
- **Posterity**: What NOT to do AT ALL (this is where courage is required)

```
| Activity | Would we start this today? | Action |
|----------|---------------------------|--------|
| [activity 1] | Yes — core | Continue, invest |
| [activity 2] | Maybe — declining | Review in 3 months |
| [activity 3] | No — legacy | Abandon |
| [activity 4] | No — distraction | Stop immediately |
```

## Practice 5: Make Effective Decisions

### The Tool: The Decision Process

Drucker's decision-making is NOT about speed. It's about doing the right things.

**Step 1: Is this a generic or unique situation?**
- **Generic**: Happens to many organizations. Apply a rule/policy. (Most situations are generic)
- **Truly unique**: Rare. Requires individual judgment.
- **Trap**: Treating a generic problem as unique (reinventing the wheel every time)

**Step 2: Define the boundary conditions**
"What is the minimum this decision must achieve? What are the specifications it must satisfy?"

If a decision doesn't satisfy boundary conditions, it's wrong — no matter how elegant.

**Step 3: Start with what is RIGHT, not what is acceptable**
"One always has to compromise in the end. But unless one starts with what is right, one ends up with the wrong compromise."

**Step 4: Build action into the decision**
A decision without an action plan is just a good intention. Define:
- Who has to know about this decision?
- What action is required?
- Who is going to do it?
- By when?

**Step 5: Build feedback into the decision**
"Executives who make effective decisions go out and look for themselves. The reason: they need organized feedback."

Don't rely on reports. Go and see (genchi genbutsu).

### Decision Quality Checklist
```
[ ] Is this generic or unique?
[ ] What are the boundary conditions (minimum acceptable outcome)?
[ ] Am I starting with what's RIGHT (not what's politically easy)?
[ ] Who needs to know? Who acts? By when?
[ ] How will I verify the decision is working? (Not reports — direct observation)
[ ] When will I review this decision? (Set a date)
```

## Weekly Effectiveness Review Template

Use every Friday (15 minutes):

```
This week:
1. TIME: Where did my time actually go? [review calendar/log]
   - Biggest time waster this week: ___
   - Action: ___

2. CONTRIBUTION: What did I contribute to results/values/people?
   - Key contribution: ___
   - What I should have contributed but didn't: ___

3. STRENGTHS: Did I work in my areas of strength?
   - Best use of strength this week: ___
   - Time spent compensating for weakness: ___

4. PRIORITIES: Did I work on first things first?
   - Priority #1 status: ___
   - What did I do that I should have said NO to: ___

5. DECISIONS: What decisions are pending?
   - Decision needed: ___
   - Boundary conditions: ___
   - Next step: ___
```
