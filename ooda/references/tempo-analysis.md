# Tempo Analysis — Competitive Speed Measurement

## What is Tempo?

In Boyd's OODA framework, **tempo** is the rate at which an actor completes OODA loops relative to competitors. Faster tempo means acting on newer information while competitors are still reacting to your old moves.

Tempo is not just "being fast." It is about **relative speed** — completing your loop faster than the other side completes theirs.

## Measuring Tempo: The OODA Speed Audit

### Per-Phase Measurement

For each OODA phase, estimate cycle time for yourself and each competitor:

| Phase | What to Measure | Typical Units |
|-------|----------------|---------------|
| **Observe** | Time from event to awareness | Hours to days |
| **Orient** | Time from awareness to understanding | Hours to weeks |
| **Decide** | Time from understanding to commitment | Minutes to months |
| **Act** | Time from commitment to execution | Hours to months |
| **Full Loop** | Event to completed response | Days to quarters |

## The Five Tempo Strategies

### 1. Accelerate Your Loop
Make each phase faster:

| Phase | Acceleration Methods |
|-------|---------------------|
| Observe | Real-time dashboards, embedded sensors, customer feedback loops, social listening |
| Orient | Diverse team (multiple lenses), pre-built mental models, war gaming experience |
| Decide | Pre-delegated authority, decision frameworks, pre-approved playbooks |
| Act | Agile sprints, continuous deployment, pre-positioned resources, standing contracts |

### 2. Slow Their Loop
Deliberately increase the competitor's cycle time:

| Phase | Disruption Methods |
|-------|-------------------|
| Observe | Information asymmetry, stealth moves, misdirection, moving faster than their sensors |
| Orient | Novelty (do something they've never seen), ambiguity, multiple simultaneous actions |
| Decide | Present them with dilemmas (no good options), force complex tradeoffs |
| Act | Regulatory capture, IP barriers, switching costs, talent acquisition |

### 3. Inside the Loop
Operate at a tempo faster than the competitor's observation capability:

```
Time ──────────────────────────────────────▶

US:     [Act1]──[Act2]──[Act3]──[Act4]──
THEM:   [Observe Act1...]──[Orient...]──[Decide...]──[React to Act1]
                                                      ↑
                                          (But we're on Act4 now)
```

When you're "inside their loop," their reactions are always to your past actions, making them perpetually late and increasingly confused.

### 4. Tempo Variation (Fast Transients)
Boyd emphasized that UNPREDICTABLE tempo is more disorienting than steady fast tempo:

- Periods of apparent inaction → sudden burst of activity
- Move slowly on one front → rapidly on another
- The shift itself creates confusion in the competitor's Orient phase

### 5. Parallel Loops
Run multiple OODA loops simultaneously on different fronts:

```
Loop 1: Product innovation    [O→O→D→A]───[O→O→D→A]───
Loop 2: Market positioning       [O→O→D→A]───[O→O→D→A]───
Loop 3: Talent acquisition    [O→O→D→A]───[O→O→D→A]───
```

Competitors who can only process one loop at a time become overwhelmed.

## Tempo Bottleneck Analysis

The slowest phase determines the overall loop speed. Identify and attack the bottleneck:

### Common Bottlenecks by Organization Type

| Org Type | Usual Bottleneck | Why | Fix |
|----------|-----------------|-----|-----|
| Startup | Act (resources) | Great ideas, no ability to execute at scale | Focus, partnerships, minimal viable everything |
| Large Corp | Decide (hierarchy) | Good data, good analysis, 6 months of committees | Decentralize authority, pre-approved playbooks |
| Government | Orient (mental models) | Outdated assumptions baked into policy | External advisors, rotation programs, red teams |
| Military | Orient → Decide (doctrine) | Doctrine is slow to update | Mission-type tactics, empowered NCOs |
| Tech Company | Observe (filter bubbles) | Echo chambers, metric fixation | Customer immersion, diverse hiring, external boards |

### Bottleneck Diagnostic

Ask these questions to find your bottleneck:

1. **Do we know what's happening?** (If no → Observe bottleneck)
2. **Do we understand what it means?** (If no → Orient bottleneck)
3. **Can we agree on what to do?** (If no → Decide bottleneck)
4. **Can we actually do it?** (If no → Act bottleneck)

## Tempo in Different Domains

### Business Competition

| Signal | Tempo Meaning |
|--------|--------------|
| Competitor copies your move within weeks | They're observing fast but not orienting (reactive, not creative) |
| Competitor doesn't react for months | Either very slow loop OR they've decided your move is irrelevant |
| Competitor makes surprising moves | They may have a better Orient than you — danger |
| Market keeps surprising you | Your Observe or Orient is too slow |

### Product Development

| Metric | Tempo Indicator |
|--------|----------------|
| Feature idea → shipped | Full loop time |
| Bug reported → fixed | Responsiveness (Act speed) |
| Customer interview → insight | Orient speed |
| Competitor launch → your response | Competitive loop time |

### Crisis Management

In crises, tempo matters enormously:
- **Golden hour**: The first response shapes everything that follows
- **Information half-life**: Facts change rapidly; old information becomes dangerous
- **Decision cost**: Delayed decisions in a crisis have exponentially increasing costs
- **Communication tempo**: Stakeholders' OODA loops also need feeding

## Measuring Your Own Tempo: Practical Methods

### Method 1: Event Tracking
Pick 3 recent events/changes. For each, track:
- When did the event happen?
- When did we first KNOW about it?
- When did we UNDERSTAND what it meant?
- When did we DECIDE what to do?
- When did we COMPLETE our response?

Calculate the gaps. That's your real tempo.

### Method 2: Decision Audit
Sample 10 recent decisions. For each, record:
- Days from trigger to decision
- Number of meetings required
- Number of approvals needed
- Days from decision to first action
- Days from first action to measurable result

### Method 3: Competitor Shadow
Track a competitor's visible actions over 6 months:
- Product launches, pricing changes, hiring, partnerships
- Estimate their loop time from observed patterns
- Compare to your own

### Method 4: Customer Lag
Measure the gap between:
- Customer need emerges → you become aware (Observe lag)
- You become aware → you understand the need (Orient lag)
- You understand → you start building (Decide lag)
- You start building → customer gets the solution (Act lag)

## Tempo Improvement Playbook

### Quick Wins (This Week)
1. Identify your single slowest phase and remove one bottleneck
2. Create a pre-approved playbook for your 3 most common decisions
3. Set up one real-time metric that you currently get weekly/monthly

### Medium-Term (This Quarter)
1. Decentralize decision authority for reversible decisions under $X
2. Establish a regular "break our model" session (Orient improvement)
3. Run a war game to build implicit guidance across the leadership team

### Strategic (This Year)
1. Build parallel OODA capability (multiple loops on different fronts)
2. Develop fast-transient capability (ability to shift tempo unexpectedly)
3. Create an institutional culture that rewards speed over perfection for reversible decisions
