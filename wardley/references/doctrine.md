# Wardley Mapping — Doctrine (Complete Reference)

Doctrine consists of universal principles that apply regardless of context. Unlike climatic patterns (external forces) or gameplay (context-dependent moves), doctrine represents good practice that any organization should follow.

Simon Wardley organizes doctrine into phases of organizational maturity.

---

## Phase I: Stop Self-Destructing

These are the basics. If you are not doing these, everything else is wasted effort.

### 1. Know your users
Identify who your users are. Not assumed users, not internal stakeholders pretending to be users — actual end users.

### 2. Focus on user needs
Anchor strategy on what users need, not what you want to sell or what technology you want to build.

### 3. Know the details
Understand the details of your value chain, your costs, your processes. You cannot make good decisions with high-level abstractions alone.

### 4. Remove bias and duplication
Multiple teams building the same thing? That is waste. Decision-making based on gut feel when data exists? That is bias.

### 5. Use appropriate methods
Agile for the uncertain. Lean for the emerging. Six Sigma for the industrialized. Not one-size-fits-all.

### 6. Think fast, inexpensive, restrained, and elegant (FIRE)
Small, focused teams. Low-cost experiments. Restraint in scope. Elegant solutions over complex ones.

### 7. Be transparent
Share maps, share plans, share failures. Hidden agendas and information hoarding are organizational cancers.

### 8. Challenge assumptions
Every assumption is a potential point of failure. "We've always done it this way" is a red flag.

---

## Phase II: Becoming More Context-Aware

### 9. Use a common language
Maps, diagrams, shared vocabulary. If people cannot communicate clearly about strategy, they cannot execute it.

### 10. Focus on high situational awareness
Know where you are before deciding where to go. Maps provide this. Frameworks without context are dangerous.

### 11. Think about aptitude and attitude
Different people thrive in different evolutionary stages. Pioneers (genesis), Settlers (product), Town Planners (commodity).

### 12. Design for constant evolution
Systems, processes, and organizations should be designed to handle change, not resist it.

### 13. Use a systematic mechanism of learning
Map, act, observe, update the map. Strategy is not a one-time exercise — it is a continuous loop.

### 14. A bias toward the new (genesis) is not always right
Shiny new thing syndrome. Sometimes the right move is to commoditize, not innovate.

### 15. Listen to your ecosystem
Suppliers, partners, competitors, and adjacent industries all provide signals about evolution and change.

### 16. A bias toward data over opinion
When data is available, use it. When it is not, be explicit that you are making an assumption.

---

## Phase III: Becoming More Strategic

### 17. Strategy is iterative, not sequential
Plan-do-check-act. Map-move-learn-remap. Not "create five-year plan, execute."

### 18. Think small (as in teams, not ambition)
Small teams move fast, communicate well, and adapt quickly. Two-pizza teams. Not 200-person programs.

### 19. Distribute power and decision making
Those closest to the problem should make decisions. Central planning of tactical execution is slow and error-prone.

### 20. Provide purpose, mastery, and autonomy
Dan Pink's motivation framework applies to strategy execution. Give teams a mission, the skills, and the freedom.

### 21. Think about the middle
Not just the bleeding edge and the commodity — the transition from product to commodity (the "war" phase) is where the most value is created and destroyed.

### 22. Use standards where appropriate
Standards reduce waste, improve interoperability, and accelerate evolution. But premature standardization kills innovation.

### 23. Manage failure
Expect failure in Genesis. Minimize failure in Commodity. Different attitudes toward failure at different stages.

### 24. Exploit the landscape
Use maps to find opportunities: gaps in the market, components about to shift, inertia in competitors.

---

## Phase IV: Continuously Adapting

### 25. Move fast
Speed of adaptation is a competitive advantage. The OODA loop applies: observe, orient, decide, act — faster than competitors.

### 26. There is no single culture
Pioneer culture (exploration, risk-taking) is different from Town Planner culture (efficiency, reliability). Both are needed.

### 27. Seek the best
Recruit and develop talent appropriate to each stage. Pioneers are not better than Town Planners — they are different.

### 28. Optimize flow
Value should flow smoothly from Genesis (pioneers discover) to Product (settlers develop) to Commodity (town planners scale).

### 29. Think big (ambition, not team size)
Small teams, big vision. Audacious goals executed through focused, iterative effort.

### 30. Be humble
The map is not the territory. Your understanding is always incomplete. Be willing to be wrong and update.

---

## Applying Doctrine

### Self-Assessment Checklist
For each doctrine principle, rate your organization:
- **Green**: We do this consistently
- **Amber**: We do this sometimes or partially
- **Red**: We do not do this

### Priority Order
1. Start with Phase I — stop self-destructing
2. Move to Phase II only when Phase I is solid
3. Phases III and IV require organizational maturity

### Common Anti-Patterns
| Anti-Pattern | Violated Doctrine |
|-------------|-------------------|
| "One methodology for everything" | #5 Use appropriate methods |
| "The CEO decides everything" | #19 Distribute power |
| Strategy created once per year | #17 Strategy is iterative |
| No one knows who the user is | #1 Know your users |
| "We need to innovate!" (without context) | #10 Situational awareness |
| Same culture everywhere | #26 No single culture |
| Large monolithic teams | #18 Think small |
| Hiding failures | #7 Be transparent |

### Pioneer-Settler-Town Planner Model
Doctrine suggests organizing teams by evolution stage:

```
Pioneer         Settler          Town Planner
(Genesis)       (Product)        (Commodity)
- Explore       - Develop        - Industrialize
- Fail fast     - Listen to      - Maximize
- High risk       customers        efficiency
- Prototype     - Iterate        - Standardize
- Small teams   - Build market   - Volume ops
```

The flow: Pioneers discover something new. Settlers take the discovery and turn it into a product. Town Planners commoditize it for mass consumption. Each hand-off is critical and often where organizations fail.
