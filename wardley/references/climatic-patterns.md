# Wardley Mapping — Climatic Patterns (Complete Reference)

Climatic patterns are external forces that act on the map. Like weather, you cannot control them but you must account for them. Simon Wardley has identified 40+ patterns, organized here by category.

---

## Category 1: Evolution & Competition

### 1. Everything evolves through supply and demand competition
Components move from Genesis to Commodity over time as competition and demand drive standardization.

### 2. Evolution consists of multiple waves (Peace/War/Wonder)
- **Peace**: Steady competition, product improvement, sustaining innovation
- **War**: Disruption point where component shifts from product to commodity/utility — brutal, fast, winner-take-most
- **Wonder**: New higher-order systems emerge on top of newly commoditized components

### 3. You cannot measure evolution over time
Evolution is not a function of time. A component may stay in Genesis for decades or rush through Product to Commodity in years.

### 4. Characteristics change as components evolve
How you manage, build, and buy a component depends on its evolution stage. What works in Genesis (experimentation) fails in Commodity (efficiency).

### 5. No single method fits all
Agile for Genesis/Custom, Lean for Product, Six Sigma for Commodity. Using the wrong method for the stage creates waste.

### 6. Components in the same stage of evolution have similar properties
Genesis components share uncertainty, rapid change, and high failure rates regardless of industry.

---

## Category 2: Inertia & Resistance

### 7. Past success breeds inertia
The more successful a company has been with an existing approach, the harder it is to change. Three types:
- **Consumer inertia**: Users reluctant to switch
- **Supplier inertia**: Vendors protecting existing business
- **Institutional inertia**: Organizational culture/process resistance

### 8. Inertia increases the more successful the past model
Market leaders are most vulnerable — they have the most to lose and the strongest resistance to change.

### 9. Inertia can kill a company
Kodak (film), Blockbuster (rental stores), Nokia (feature phones) — all had the resources but not the will to adapt.

### 10. Efficiency does not mean effectiveness
You can be extremely efficient at doing the wrong thing. Optimizing a component that should be eliminated is a common inertia trap.

---

## Category 3: Value & Creation

### 11. Higher order systems create new sources of value
When electricity became a commodity, it enabled factories. When compute became a utility (cloud), it enabled SaaS, AI, etc.

### 12. Efficiency enables innovation
Commoditization of lower-order components frees resources and attention for higher-order innovation.

### 13. Capital flows to new areas of value
Investment gravitates toward Genesis/Custom where returns are highest (and risk is highest).

### 14. Creative destruction
New entrants leveraging commoditized components destroy incumbents stuck on previous product-stage business models.

### 15. Value moves up the chain
As components commoditize, competitive differentiation and profit shift to higher-order activities.

---

## Category 4: Co-evolution & Ecosystems

### 16. Evolution creates co-evolution
Change in one component forces adaptation in connected components. Cloud computing forced changes in deployment, security, monitoring.

### 17. Co-evolution of practice
New practices emerge with evolution (DevOps co-evolved with cloud/containers).

### 18. Ecosystems form around evolved components
Commodity/utility components attract ecosystems of complementary products and services.

### 19. Supply and demand competition drives commoditization
If enough people need something, competitors emerge, standards form, and the component commoditizes.

### 20. De facto standards emerge through competition
One implementation wins (TCP/IP over OSI, x86 over RISC in PCs, then ARM in mobile).

---

## Category 5: Market & Strategic Dynamics

### 21. Red Queen effect
You must keep evolving just to maintain your position. Standing still means falling behind.

### 22. Punctuated equilibrium
Long periods of stability are interrupted by rapid change (war phase). Most strategic planning assumes continuous change — wrong.

### 23. The exchange from product to utility is inevitable
If a component provides standard value and can be delivered at scale, it will eventually become a utility.

### 24. Commoditization does not equal low value
Electricity is a commodity — it is also essential. Commoditization means standardized delivery, not unimportant.

### 25. Evolution of a communication mechanism changes the community
Email changed business communication. Social media changed marketing. AI will change knowledge work.

### 26. Competitors can co-operate for mutual benefit
Co-opetition: competing firms may jointly commoditize shared infrastructure while competing on higher-order offerings.

---

## Category 6: Prediction & Weak Signals

### 27. Weak signals precede evolution shifts
Publications, startup activity, open-source projects, conference talks — these signal that a war phase is coming.

### 28. There is a gap between perception and reality
An organization may believe it is innovative while most of its components are product/commodity. Maps expose this gap.

### 29. Not everything can be predicted, but direction can be anticipated
You cannot predict exactly when something will commoditize, but you can see that it will.

### 30. Evolution progresses from rare to ubiquitous
Genesis = rare, expensive, poorly understood. Commodity = ubiquitous, cheap, well-defined.

---

## Category 7: Organizational & Structural

### 31. Structure must follow strategy (which follows landscape)
Org design should reflect the evolution stage of your components. Skunkworks for Genesis, factory for Commodity.

### 32. There is no core
What feels like your "core competency" today may be a commodity tomorrow. Don't anchor identity to a component.

### 33. The speed of evolution is not uniform
Some components evolve fast (software), others slow (construction). Your strategy must account for different speeds.

### 34. Evolution of one component changes the landscape for all
When AWS commoditized compute, it changed the strategic landscape for every software company.

### 35. Outsourcing vs insourcing depends on evolution
Commodities should be outsourced/consumed as utility. Genesis/Custom should be built in-house.

---

## Category 8: User & Demand

### 36. User needs are the anchor
Everything starts with a user need. A map without a user need at the top is a map without purpose.

### 37. Users' needs change over time
As components evolve and higher-order systems emerge, user expectations shift upward.

### 38. Unmet needs exist at every level
Even in mature markets, there are unmet needs — especially at the intersection of commoditized and novel components.

### 39. Demand can be created through co-evolution
New enabling components create demand for things that didn't previously exist (smartphones created demand for apps).

---

## Category 9: Competition & Gameplay

### 40. First mover advantage diminishes with evolution
In Genesis, being first matters. In Commodity, execution and scale matter more.

### 41. Fast followers exploit pioneers' learning
Let pioneers validate the market, then execute faster with their learnings. Viable strategy in Custom/Product stages.

### 42. The landscape is constantly changing
Any strategy based on a static view will fail. Continuous mapping is required.

### 43. Competitor actions are often predictable
If you know where competitors' components sit on the evolution axis, you can predict their likely moves and inertia points.

### 44. Open approaches accelerate evolution
Open-source, open standards, and open data accelerate the shift from Product to Commodity (a deliberate strategic play).

---

## Using Climatic Patterns

When analyzing a Wardley Map:
1. For each component, ask: "Which climatic patterns apply here?"
2. Look for components where evolution is being resisted (inertia)
3. Identify where efficiency gains could enable new innovation
4. Check for co-evolution signals — when one component moves, what else must change?
5. Scan for weak signals of upcoming war phases
